What is a

PRIVACY POLICY?






Why do we

NEED THEM?

It's required by law. Since people increasingly value their privacy, a privacy policy is essential to enforce transparency and build trust between organizations and consumers.






What do

THEY CONTAIN?

It depends on the nature of the business, their location, the laws applicable to them, among many other factors. However, the following practice categories are fairly standard and can be found in most privacy policies.

First Party Collection

Describes data collection or data use by the company/organization owning the product or service.

Third Party Sharing

Describes data sharing with third parties or data collection by third parties.

Data Security

Describes how usersâ€™ information is secured and protected, e.g., from confidentiality, integrity, or availability breaches.

User Access, Edit & Deletion

Describes practices that allow users to access, edit or delete the data that the company/organization has about them.

Data Retention

Describes practices that specify the retention period for collected user information.

User Choice/Control

Describes general choices and control options available to users.

Policy Change

Describes practices concerning if and how users will be informed of changes to its privacy policy, including any choices offered to users.

Do Not Track

Describes if and how Do Not Track signals (DNT) for online tracking and advertising are honored.

Specific Audiences

Specific audiences, such as children or international users, for which the company/organization may provide special provisions.

Other

Any miscellaneous practices that are not covered in the above categories.

Privacy Policy Structure

Privacy policies are made up of smaller sections or paragraphs, called "segments". Each of these segments, within a company's privacy policy, provides information about one or more of the aforementioned privacy practice categories.

MSDS DS5500 Capstone

This project was developed as part of a final Capstone under the Northeastern University MSDS program. It provides regulators and consumers a platform to quickly view and analyze a higher level and a more granular break down of data practices involved in privacy policies.

By leveraging modern machine learning and natural language processing techniques, we were able to classify policy segments into pre-defined data practice categories. In addition, we were able to implement a freeform Question-Answering system to enable quick extraction of salient information from these documents.
